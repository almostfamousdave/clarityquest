Ignorance vs. Stupidity in the Age of Television News

1. Definitions

  • Ignorance is a lack of knowledge or information. It is curable through education, experience, or exposure.
  • Stupidity is a learned or ingrained incapacity or unwillingness to think critically, apply logic, or change one’s mind in the face of new evidence.

Key Insight: Television, especially around-the-clock opinionated news, doesn’t just fail to cure ignorance — it can actively cultivate stupidity.

2. The Medium is the Message (McLuhan & Postman)

  • Television prioritizes emotion, speed, and visual impact.
  • Complex ideas are reduced to soundbites, stripped of nuance.
  • As Postman warns, televised content turns all subjects — even war or elections — into entertainment.

Result: The brain is trained to expect easy answers, strong feelings, and fast conclusions. This undermines the slower, more effortful processes required for learning and wisdom.

3. The Rise of 24-Hour Opinionated News

  • News-as-entertainment is a profit model, not a public service.
  • Pundits fill time with speculation, outrage, and tribal narratives.
  • This encourages confirmation bias, not curiosity.
  • Facts become subordinate to feelings.

Consequence: Viewers are saturated with emotion but starved for truth. Repetition replaces learning. Over time, this builds a pseudo-knowledge that feels confident but is often shallow or false.

4. From Ignorant to Unteachable

  • Someone who is ignorant can be taught.
  • Someone who is “news-fed stupid”:
    • Thinks they already know.
    • Confuses emotional certainty for truth.
    • Rejects conflicting facts as fake or biased.
    • Responds to challenges with slogans, not thinking.

Bottom Line: This isn’t just a personal problem — it’s a societal vulnerability. A population that cannot distinguish between truth and feeling is easily manipulated.

5. Path Forward

  • Media literacy must become a core educational priority.
  • People must be taught how to think, not what to feel.
  • Long-form reading, civil debate, and real-world experiences are antidotes to televised stupidity.

As Postman said:
“The problem is not that television presents us with entertaining subject matter, but that all subject matter is presented as entertainment.”
